Concord Grape · Modified Munson System

Keeping the Renewal Zone Stocked

A guide to always having enough cane candidates for your 4 fruiting canes

📖 Key terms
Term Definition
Shoot A green, actively growing stem in the current season. Shoots have leaves and may carry fruit clusters. At the end of the season they harden off and become canes.
Cane A shoot that has matured and hardened through the dormant season. Canes are brown and woody. On Concord, only one-year-old canes (last season's shoots) produce fruiting wood — older canes will not fruit again.
Fruiting cane A selected one-year-old cane that is tied along the arm wire for the coming season. It carries 8–12 buds, each of which will push a shoot that may bear fruit. It is fully removed the following winter.
Spur A short stub of one-year-old wood cut back to just 2 buds. It is not meant to fruit directly — its job is to grow 2 new shoots during the season, giving you fresh cane candidates close to the head for the following winter's selection.
Renewal spur A spur positioned as close as possible to the head/trunk. Keeping renewal spurs near the head ensures that next year's cane candidates originate at the correct position — preventing the renewal zone from creeping outward along the wire year by year.
Head / renewal zone The compact knob of woody tissue at the top of the trunk, at arm-wire height. This is where all cuts are made each winter and where spurs and new cane bases should always originate. On Modified Munson it must sit exactly at arm-wire level — not above it.
Proximal Closer to the trunk/head. When choosing between two shoots on a spur stub, the proximal one is the one nearer the vine's center. This is the one you cut back to a renewal spur — keeping it close preserves the head's position.
Distal Further from the trunk/head. The distal shoot on a spur stub is the one that extends further along the wire — this becomes the fruiting cane for the coming season.
Bud A compressed growing point on a cane. Each bud can push one or more shoots in spring. When you prune a cane to "8–12 buds," you are counting the number of buds remaining above the base cut.
Blind bud A bud that fails to push a shoot in spring — common on very thin canes or at the very base of some canes. One reason to select pencil-thick, well-matured canes is to minimize blind buds.
The core problem: Concord is cane-pruned — it only fruits on new shoots from one-year-old wood. Every winter you replace all four fruiting canes entirely. If you don't deliberately maintain renewal spurs at the head, you'll run out of well-positioned candidates and be forced to use weak, distant, or poorly-placed canes. This guide explains the spur strategy that keeps you fully stocked every year.

Spray timing: For precise fungicide and dormant-oil timing, check NEWA (newa.cornell.edu) — it uses your local weather station data and predicts infection events better than a fixed calendar.
0 Years 1–2 — building the trunk Before renewal spurs begin

This guide picks up at Year 3, when the trunk is at wire height and renewal spur work begins. If you are planting new vines or renovating after a trunk cutback, the two prior years follow a simple rule:

Years 1–2: single trunk to arm-wire height

  1. Select one strong shoot and tie it vertically to the post every 10–12 inches as it grows. Remove all competing shoots at the base.
  2. Remove all laterals below the future head — any side shoot that emerges below arm-wire height should be rubbed or pinched off. You want all the vine's energy going into extending the single trunk upward, not branching out prematurely.
  3. Once the shoot reaches arm-wire height, pinch the tip to encourage the head to form there. If it doesn't reach the wire in Year 1, do not cut it back — just let it continue growing in Year 2 and pinch when it arrives.

Reference: Cornell Cooperative Extension guidelines; Tom Zabadal's Modified Munson demonstration videos cover this establishment phase in detail for Concord-type varieties.

1 Why canes must be renewed every year Background

Concord grapes only produce fruit on new shoots that grow from buds on wood that is exactly one year old. Once a cane has fruited, it will not produce again — it becomes spent wood. This is why the entire canopy of canes must be replaced each dormant season.

Grape vines fruit on new shoots growing from the previous year's canes. Once a cane has produced, it will not produce again. — Image: Deep Green Permaculture

This means your pruning has two equally important jobs each winter: selecting this year's 4 fruiting canes, and setting up next year's supply of replacement canes via renewal spurs. If you only think about the fruiting canes, you'll find yourself short of good candidates the following winter.

2 Year 3 — establishing your first renewal spurs Third winter pruning

By the third winter, your trunk has reached arm-wire height and you have a cluster of shoots at the head to choose from. This is the first time you establish the renewal-spur structure that you'll maintain forever.

What to do at the head — year 3

  1. Select 2 fruiting canes per side (4 total). Each should be roughly pencil-thick, growing from as close to the trunk as possible, with buds spaced no more than a fist-width apart. Prune each to 8–12 buds and tie along the arm wire.
  2. Leave 1 renewal spur per side (2 total). Pick a shoot close to the trunk base, on the opposite side from or just below each fruiting cane, and cut it back to just 2 buds. These 2-bud stubs are your renewal spurs — they will grow the candidates for next year's canes.
  3. Remove everything else cleanly back to the trunk.

Year 3: two fruiting canes per side are tied along the wires, and one renewal spur per side is cut back to 2 buds. — Image: Deep Green Permaculture

Head position warning

For your Modified Munson system, the head must be at arm-wire height — not above it. Every cut must be made back to the head, never an inch higher. If you leave a spur stub above the wire, the head migrates upward each year and your renewal zone ends up stranded above the trellis.
2½ Scaling from 2 spurs to 4 — what happens between Year 3 and Year 4 Growing season + fourth winter

Year 3 winter leaves you with only 2 renewal spurs — one per side. This is intentional: the head is newly formed and you had limited shoots to work with. The move to 4 spurs happens naturally over the growing season that follows and the pruning that comes after it.

How it unfolds — step by step

  1. During Year 3's growing season: Each of your 2 renewal spurs has 2 buds. Both buds push shoots, so by fall that single spur stub is carrying 2 shoots. Across the whole vine you now have 4 spur shoots total. Additionally, basal shoots will emerge from near the base of each of your 4 fruiting canes — these are the raw material for the second spur position on each side.
  2. Year 4 winter — left side (repeat identically for right):
    • New spur 1 — from last year's renewal spur stub: it grew 2 shoots. Take the lower or inner one and cut it to 2 buds. This is your first new spur. Take the other shoot and extend it as a fruiting cane (8–12 buds, tied to wire).
    • New spur 2 — from basal growth near your second fruiting cane: find the best short shoot growing right at the base of the second cane's attachment point at the head. Cut it to 2 buds. This is your second spur on this side.
  3. Result: 2 spurs per side = 4 renewal spurs total. This is the number you maintain forever from this point onward.

What if you can't find a second basal shoot in Year 4?

A young head may not throw abundant basal growth in its first full season. If you can't find a well-placed shoot for spur 2 on one side, stay at 2 spurs for that side for one more year — it costs you nothing except having fewer backup candidates. By Year 5 the head will be more established and basal shoots will be plentiful. Don't stretch to use a poorly placed shoot just to hit the target number; a badly positioned spur is worse than no spur.
3 Year 4 — the renewal pattern becomes permanent Fourth winter pruning

By year 4, your renewal spurs from last winter have each grown 2 shoots. Those shoots are now one year old and are your best cane candidates — well-positioned, close to the head, and vigorous. This is the pattern you follow every single year from here on.

Year 4: new canes selected from last year's renewal spurs are tied as fruiting canes. New renewal spurs are set from shoots close to the trunk. — Image: Deep Green Permaculture

⚠ Note: this diagram shows the more distal shoot being selected as the renewal spur while the more proximal shoot is chosen as the fruiting cane. This is the reverse of what you should do — spuring distally causes the renewal zone to creep outward along the wire each year. Always spur the proximal shoot and extend the distal shoot as the fruiting cane.

The permanent annual 3-step rule (year 4 onward)

  1. Prune last year's 2-bud renewal spurs in half — cut off the top shoot (the stronger or more distal one) and discard it. You're left with a single shoot on each spur stub — this becomes a fruiting cane candidate.
  2. Select your 4 fruiting canes from the available shoots (2 per side). Ideally use the shoots that grew from the renewal spurs — they're closest to the head. Prune each to 8–12 buds and tie to the arm wire.
  3. Set 4 new renewal spurs (2 per side). For each fruiting cane you've selected, find the best nearby shoot at the base — or the lower shoot remaining on the old spur — and cut it back hard to just 2 buds. These are your renewal spurs for next year.

First "Year 5+" diagram omitted — contains a critical error for renewal zone management

The source article's first Year 5+ diagram shows the more proximal (inner) shoot being selected as the fruiting cane and the more distal (outer) shoot being cut back to a 2-bud spur. This is backwards for anyone trying to prevent the renewal zone from creeping outward. If you spur the distal shoot, next year's cane candidates originate further from the head than this year's — the zone drifts away from the trunk a little each season.

The correct rule: always spur the more proximal shoot (closer to the trunk/head) and use the more distal shoot as the fruiting cane. The spur stays close in; the long cane extends outward. This way next year's candidates are always at least as close to the head as this year's, keeping the renewal zone compact.

5 What to do when you don't have enough good candidates Troubleshooting

Even with renewal spurs in place, some winters you'll find the shoots from them are weak, blind (no buds), or too far from the head. Here's how to handle each scenario.

Problem Cause Fix
Renewal spur shot only one weak shoot Low vigor, shading, or spur was cut too late Prune it back to 2 buds again and use a nearby basal shoot from an old cane stub as your fruiting cane this year instead.
All 4 spur shoots are too spindly (thinner than a pencil) Vine overcropped or underfed; too many canes last year Use the thickest available. Reduce total bud count to 30–40 this season. Assess irrigation and fertility.
Best shoots are 12–18" from the head Renewal spurs were left too far out on old canes Use them this year but set your new renewal spurs from the basal-most shoots available — even if they're weak — to begin pulling the zone back in. Takes 2–3 seasons.
No spur shoots at all — forgot to set spurs last winter Only fruiting canes were selected, no spurs left Find the lowest-placed shoots growing directly from old cane bases near the trunk. Use these as both canes and set new spurs from their bases. Don't repeat the mistake.
Head has crept 12"+ above the arm wire Cuts made above the head for several years in a row Trunk renovation needed — see the hard cutback / gradual replacement method. See previous diagram guide.

Cane selection priority — always in this order

  1. Shoots from renewal spurs at the head — closest to trunk, best wood. Use these first.
  2. Basal shoots from old cane stubs near the head — second choice if spur shoots are weak.
  3. Shoots that grew from buds on last year's fruiting canes, close to the trunk — acceptable but further from head; set a new spur from the lowest one to begin pulling back.
  4. Never use shoots more than 6–8" from the head unless no other option exists. The head will migrate and you'll have the creeping-trunk problem within a few seasons.

☀ Growing season — shoot positioning and thinning June–July

Winter pruning alone is not enough on the Modified Munson. The system's divided-curtain canopy only delivers good airflow and light penetration if you actively manage shoots during the growing season. Skipping this step significantly increases black rot and downy mildew pressure regardless of how well you pruned in winter.

What to do, June–July

  1. Tuck shoots downward between the two inner wires. As shoots grow from your fruiting canes, guide them between the parallel inner wires so they hang in a neat downward curtain on each side. The wires hold them in place without tying.
  2. Remove lateral shoots crowding the fruiting zone. Secondary shoots (laterals) growing from the base of leaves on your main shoots should be pinched or removed where they're dense — particularly in the cluster zone. This opens the canopy to spray penetration and air movement.
  3. Don't over-strip. Some basal laterals near the renewal zone can be left short — they may be useful as cane candidates if the spur shoots underperform.

Reference: Cornell Cooperative Extension bulletin "Shoot Positioning Native American Grapevines" covers this practice in detail for Concord-type varieties on divided-canopy systems.
