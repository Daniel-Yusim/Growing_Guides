⚠️ Black Knot Emergency — Plum Trunk

Trunk infection is the most critical situation in this guide. Assess before anything else.

Step 1 — Assess girdling: Examine each knot on the trunk. Estimate what percentage of the trunk circumference is encircled by active knotted tissue.

  • More than 50% girdled: The tree is almost certainly doomed. Remove the entire tree including as many roots as possible. A dying, infected trunk is an inoculum factory spreading spores to neighboring plums and cherries every season. Destroy all removed material — bag it and send to trash, or burn if permitted. Do not compost.
  • Less than 50% girdled: Surgical removal may save the tree. Cut 4–6 inches below the visible margin of each knot, well into clean white wood. Sterilize pruning tools with 70% isopropyl alcohol between every single cut. Paint wounds immediately with copper fungicide paste or wound sealant.
  • All removed material — branches, chips, sawdust — must be bagged and discarded in trash or burned. Never compost.
  • After surgery: Spray copper + myclobutanil throughout the season. The critical infection window for new black knot is from bud swell through 3–4 weeks post-bloom. Do not miss sprays during this period.
  • Monitor closely: If new knots appear on the trunk next season, remove the tree. Sprays suppress new infections on branches but do not kill established knots.
Cultural note on cherries Black knot (Apiosporina morbosa) also affects sour cherries — it shares the same host range as plum. With a heavily infected plum tree in close proximity, the cherry tree is at meaningfully elevated risk from airborne spores. Inspect all cherry branches carefully now while dormant and remove any black knots found, using the same 4–6 inch margin cut-below-visible-knot rule. Cherry is generally less severely affected than plum, but infections will worsen year over year without management. Include cherry in the copper + myclobutanil black knot spray program from bud swell through 3–4 weeks post-bloom.

🛒 Products — What I Have & What to Buy

On hand

Sevin Insect Killer On Hand

Modern Sevin contains zeta-cypermethrin (pyrethroid), not carbaryl. Effective against Japanese beetles, Spotted Lanternfly, Eastern tent caterpillar, cherry slug/sawfly.

⚑ Never apply during bloom — kills pollinators. PHI: 3 days on most fruits; check label for your formulation. Health & safety: Toxic to bees and aquatic invertebrates — never spray near water or during bloom. Toxic to cats (pyrethroid). Wear gloves and avoid skin contact with concentrate. Wash hands and any exposed skin thoroughly after use.

Bifenthrin 7.9% On Hand

Pyrethroid with longer residual activity than Sevin. A versatile and important tool across your entire orchard: Spotted Lanternfly adults on grapes; Plum Curculio at petal fall (all stone fruits + apple); Codling moth and Apple maggot on apple; Peachtree Borer trunk spray (July–August); Brown Marmorated Stink Bug; Japanese beetles.

⚑ PHI: 7 days on most fruits — verify on the label. Never during bloom. For peachtree borer, saturate lower 18 inches of trunk + root flare. Rotate with Sevin. Caution: pyrethroids at petal fall can harm predatory mites — scout and treat with oil if needed. Health & safety: Bifenthrin is extremely toxic to fish, aquatic invertebrates, and bees — never apply near water, storm drains, or during bloom. It is also toxic to cats, who lack the liver enzymes to metabolize pyrethroids. Keep cats out of treated areas until sprays are fully dry. Wear gloves and eye protection; avoid skin contact with concentrate.

Insecticidal Soap On Hand

Contact kill of soft-bodied insects and mites. Primary uses: currant aphid on Ribes; spider mite flare-ups after pyrethroid use; scale crawlers. Much gentler on beneficial insects than pyrethroids.

⚑ Must hit pest directly — cover leaf undersides. Typically 2–3 fl oz/gallon. No PHI on edibles. Do not apply above 90°F. No residual protection.

Malathion On Hand — Backup / Ribes

Organophosphate insecticide. Most relevant use: Spotted Wing Drosophila (SWD) on cherries and Ribes near harvest (SWD lays eggs in soft, ripe fruit). Also backup for currant aphid and leafhoppers.

⚑ PHI: 1 day on Ribes (key SWD use), 3–7 days on tree fruits — check label. Do not use near water. More toxic to beneficials than soap. Health: Malathion is an organophosphate that inhibits cholinesterase — the enzyme that controls nerve signaling. Skin absorption is significant. Always wear chemical-resistant gloves, long sleeves, eye protection, and a respirator. If you experience headache, nausea, excessive sweating, or blurred vision after application, seek medical attention immediately. Do not spray near water sources — malathion is highly toxic to fish and aquatic invertebrates.

Daconil / Fungonil On Hand

Active ingredient: chlorothalonil. Broad-spectrum protectant for brown rot, cherry leaf spot, grape diseases, apple scab, Ribes leaf diseases. Good backbone product for cover spray rotations.

⚑ NOT effective against Cedar-Apple Rust or powdery mildew. Avoid spraying open flowers. 7-day PHI on stone fruits, apples, grapes. Health: Chlorothalonil is classified by the EPA as a probable human carcinogen with repeated exposure. Always wear a properly fitted respirator (N95 minimum; P100 preferred), chemical-resistant gloves, and eye protection when mixing and applying. Do not breathe spray mist. Wash all exposed skin thoroughly after use.

Mancozeb On Hand

EBDC protectant. Excellent for grape black rot, apple scab, stone fruit diseases, Ribes anthracnose. Rotate with other fungicides to manage resistance.

⚑ CRITICAL: 66-day PHI on grapes. 7-day PHI on stone fruits and Ribes. Cannot be used alone against powdery mildew or rust. Health: Mancozeb (an EBDC fungicide) has been linked to potential thyroid and reproductive effects with prolonged or repeated exposure. Wear a dust/mist respirator, gloves, and eye protection when mixing the wettable powder — the dry powder is the highest exposure risk. Avoid breathing dust or spray mist.

Copper Fungicide — Captain Jack's On Hand

Contains copper octanoate (copper soap) at 10% — gentler than copper hydroxide. Critical for peach leaf curl (dormant only), plum and cherry black knot suppression, Ribes leaf diseases at emergence, and fall dormant sprays on all stone fruits and grapes.

⚑ Dormant rate: 2 fl oz/gallon. Reduce to 1 fl oz/gallon once living tissue is exposed. Never mix with lime sulfur — wait at least 2 weeks between them. Shake before measuring; copper settles. Use within 24 hours of mixing. Health: Copper is an eye and skin irritant — wear splash-proof goggles and gloves. The liquid concentrate stains clothing, skin, and surfaces blue-green permanently. Repeated ingestion of copper is toxic; wash hands before eating or drinking after handling.

Myclobutanil — Spectracide Immunox On Hand

The only product in this guide that controls Cedar-Apple Rust. Also excellent for grape black rot and powdery mildew, brown rot on stone fruits, black knot suppression on plum and cherry, and anthracnose / powdery mildew on Ribes. Systemic with ~72-hour curative window.

⚑ Typical rate: 1 fl oz/gallon (check the label). 7-day PHI on fruits. Replaces sulfur as primary PM tool on American-type grapes. Rotate with other modes of action to prevent resistance. Health: Myclobutanil is a sterol-synthesis inhibitor with potential endocrine-disrupting properties at high exposure levels. Wear gloves and eye protection. Avoid inhaling spray mist. The Immunox home formulation is relatively low concentration, but routine precautions still apply.

Bonide All Seasons Horticultural Oil On Hand

Dormant use: smothers overwintering scale, mite eggs, and aphid eggs on all crops. Growing-season use: powdery mildew suppression on American-type grapes and Ribes. No fungicidal activity against disease spores — cannot substitute for lime sulfur.

⚑ Do NOT apply within 2 weeks of captan or sulfur — phytotoxicity both directions. Do not use above 90°F. Growing-season rate ~1–2%; dormant rate ~3–4% — check label.

Neem Oil On Hand — Limited Use

Some fungicidal activity against powdery mildew and soft-bodied insect repellence. Useful as a mild supplement on Ribes. Less effective than myclobutanil for mildew, less effective than insecticidal soap for aphids — treat as a secondary tool only.

⚑ Apply to actively growing foliage only — no benefit on dormant wood. Do not apply in full sun or above 85°F. 0-day PHI (check label). Do not mix with captan. Strong odor.

Still needed — purchase before the season progresses

Lime Sulfur Buy Soon — Must Order Online or in Bulk

Highly effective dormant spray for overwintering disease inoculum on stone fruits and grapes. Apply before bud swell in late winter. Separate from copper by at least 2 weeks.

⚠ Serious eye hazard: lime sulfur can cause permanent blindness on contact. Full goggles (not just safety glasses) and a face shield are mandatory — do not spray in windy conditions. Also causes severe skin burns; wear long sleeves, gloves, and cover all exposed skin. The hydrogen sulfide odor is extremely pungent — wear a respirator rated for acid gases.

⚑ Retail stores typically do not carry lime sulfur — it has been discontinued from most garden center shelves. Purchase from agricultural supply companies (Tractor Supply, AG stores) or online in quart/gallon quantities. Bonide Lime Sulfur Spray is the most widely available home-use formulation. Never mix with copper — apply at least 2 weeks apart. Lime sulfur as a dormant spray (before leaf emergence) is generally safe on American-type grapes; avoid once leaves emerge.

Wettable Sulfur Buy — European Grapes Only

e.g., Bonide Sulfur Plant Fungicide. For powdery mildew on European (vinifera) grapes during the growing season. Do not use on American-type grapes or peaches.

⚑ Do not apply above 85–90°F. Keep away from peaches and nectarines. Do not apply within 2 weeks of any oil spray. Health: Sulfur dust and spray mist are respiratory irritants — wear a dust/mist respirator, eye protection, and gloves. People with sulfur sensitivity or asthma should take extra precautions. Avoid breathing any sulfur dust when measuring the wettable powder.

Captan 50WP Recommended

Excellent broad-spectrum protectant for stone fruits (brown rot) and apple. Complements mancozeb well in resistance management rotations. Good pre-harvest fungicide for peaches and cherries.

⚑ 7-day PHI on stone fruits and apple. Do not mix with horticultural oil — wait 2 weeks in either direction. Do not mix with alkaline products. Health: Captan 50WP is classified as a probable human carcinogen (EPA Group B2) based on animal studies. The wettable powder form poses the highest inhalation risk — always wear a properly fitted respirator when measuring and mixing the dry powder, along with gloves and eye protection. Do not breathe dust or spray mist.

Permethrin Concentrate Recommended — Peachtree Borer

e.g., Hi-Yield 38 Plus or Bonide Eight Insect Control. Your bifenthrin 7.9% can serve this role, but a dedicated higher-concentration permethrin product is useful for thorough peachtree borer trunk drenches on peach, nectarine, cherry, and plum in July–August.

⚑ Apply as a directed trunk spray saturating the lower 18 inches + root flare. Two applications 2–3 weeks apart starting mid-July. PHI = 14 days on stone fruits. Label must specifically mention "peach tree borer" and "trunk application." Health & safety: Permethrin is extremely toxic to cats — cats exposed to wet permethrin or who groom fur contaminated with it can suffer severe neurological toxicity and death. Keep cats away from treated areas until sprays are completely dry. Also highly toxic to fish and aquatic life — do not apply near water. Wear gloves and eye protection.

Dinotefuran Optional — Heavy SLF

e.g., Gordon's Zylam. Systemic insecticide applied as a bark spray on grape trunks for systemic SLF control. Reserve for seasons of very heavy SLF pressure when the bifenthrin + Sevin rotation is not providing adequate knockdown.

⚑ Check label for grape PHI. More expensive and harder to find than bifenthrin; treat as a backup escalation tool only.

Thiophanate-Methyl Optional — Black Knot

e.g., Bonide Fung-onil Systemic (different from Fungonil contact). Systemic fungicide with efficacy against black knot on plum and cherry. Use in combination with copper during the bud swell–post-bloom suppression window.

⚑ Rotate with copper and myclobutanil; do not rely on it alone. Check the label for specific PHI on the crop being treated.
Mixing rules — never violate these Never mix copper + lime sulfur in the same application (toxic reaction, severe phytotoxicity). Apply them at least 2 weeks apart. Never apply wettable sulfur or lime sulfur within 2 weeks of any oil spray. Mancozeb + myclobutanil is a good tank mix (protectant + systemic). Sevin is generally compatible with most fungicides, but always read both labels before mixing anything.

🍇 Grape Sulfur Guide: European vs. American Types

Why this matters American-type grapes (Vitis labrusca: Concord, Mars, Diamond, Niagara, Catawba, and labrusca hybrids) are more susceptible to sulfur phytotoxicity than European (V. vinifera) grapes. Spraying wettable sulfur on American types causes interveinal leaf scorch and can damage berries. The good news: American grapes are also naturally more resistant to powdery mildew — so they need less intensive management anyway. Use myclobutanil and/or horticultural oil instead of sulfur on your American vines.
✓ Sulfur OK

European Grapes (V. vinifera)

e.g., Chardonnay, Cabernet, Riesling, Pinot Noir, Merlot, Muscat, etc.

  • Wettable sulfur is the primary powdery mildew tool — apply every 10–14 days during shoot growth
  • Do not apply sulfur when temperatures exceed 85–90°F (phytotoxic at high heat even on vinifera)
  • Apply in early morning or late evening on hot days
  • Stop sulfur at least 3 weeks before harvest (affects wine flavor/fermentation)
  • Do not apply within 2 weeks of any oil spray
  • Vinifera are highly susceptible to both downy and powdery mildew — do not skip sprays
  • Lime sulfur dormant spray: OK
✗ No Sulfur

American Grapes (V. labrusca & hybrids)

e.g., Concord, Mars, Diamond, Niagara, Catawba, Delaware, and many French-American hybrids

  • Do not use wettable sulfur during the growing season — causes interveinal scorch on leaves and can crack berries
  • Use myclobutanil (Immunox) as your primary powdery mildew fungicide instead
  • Use horticultural / stylet oil as a complement to myclobutanil for mildew (not within 2 weeks of captan)
  • American types have natural resistance to powdery mildew — spray intervals can be slightly extended vs. European types in low-pressure years
  • They remain susceptible to black rot and downy mildew — maintain mancozeb + myclobutanil program
  • Lime sulfur as a dormant spray (before leaf emergence) is generally acceptable — but switch to copper for any applications once growth begins
If you have hybrid grapes (French-American crosses) Hybrids vary widely in sulfur sensitivity — there is no single answer for all cultivars. Conservative approach: treat them like American types and avoid sulfur. If you know a specific hybrid is tolerant, test a small area first before full application. Varieties like Vidal, Cayuga, Seyval, and Chambourcin tend to be less sensitive than pure labrusca, but sensitivity still varies. When in doubt, use myclobutanil + oil as your powdery mildew program.

🪲 Insect Pest Profiles

Why Sevin alone has been insufficient Modern Sevin (zeta-cypermethrin) is a pyrethroid — effective for many pests but with a shorter residual and somewhat lower knockdown on plum curculio than older carbaryl-based Sevin. Your bifenthrin 7.9% is a longer-residual pyrethroid and covers nearly all the pests below. Adding timed, targeted sprays beyond your general cover spray schedule is the key to protecting fruit.

Plum Curculio

Affects: sour cherry, peach/nectarine, plum, apple (all stone fruits + pome)
Crescent-shaped scars on young fruit = female egg-laying wound. On peaches: clear to amber gummy ooze at puncture sites on fruit, small stunted fruit, heavy premature fruit drop — this is the primary cause of the peach gummosis symptoms you've observed. Cat-facing distortions. Internal larvae in fallen fruit.
Biology Small brown snout weevil. Overwinters in leaf litter and woodland edges. Adults emerge at petal fall, migrate into the orchard, and immediately begin feeding and laying eggs in newly set fruit. One primary generation per year in NJ; activity concentrated in the first 3–4 weeks after petal fall. Why control failed with Sevin alone Plum curculio requires a well-timed petal-fall spray as the primary intervention. Missing or delaying this spray by even a few days results in significant damage. Bifenthrin has longer residual than modern Sevin and is a better primary tool for this pest. Management
  • Petal fall spray: Apply bifenthrin immediately at petal fall on all stone fruits and apple — this is the single most critical insecticide timing of the year for fruit protection. A second application 10–14 days later provides an important second layer.
  • Apply in the evening (PC adults are most active at night when temps exceed 70°F).
  • Remove and destroy fallen fruit throughout the season — larvae in dropped fruit complete development in the soil, adding to next year's population.
  • Clean up woodland-edge brush, rock walls, and leaf piles near the orchard — these are overwintering habitat.
⏱ Critical spray window: petal fall → 3–4 weeks after petal fall (approximately mid-May through early June in NJ)

Peachtree Borer — preventive management; secondary concern

Affects: peach, nectarine, cherry, plum (all stone fruits)
Gummy sap mixed with granular frass (sawdust-like material) at the base of the trunk near or below the soil line — not on fruit. Note: ooze appearing on the fruit surface is typically caused by plum curculio, not this pest. Bark gummosis at the trunk base, yellowing foliage, or reduced vigor are borer signs.
Biology Day-flying clearwing moth resembling a wasp. Adults emerge June–September; peak egg-laying in NJ is late July through August. Larvae bore under bark at the base of the trunk and root flare, tunneling through the cambium. One or two borers can harm growth and fruiting; several can kill a young tree in a single season. They are essentially unreachable by insecticide once inside the bark — prevention is everything. Two species Greater peachtree borer (attacks trunk base / crown, most damaging) and lesser peachtree borer (attacks higher on trunk and scaffold limbs). Treat for both with the same program. Management
  • Trunk spray — bifenthrin: Apply bifenthrin 7.9% as a directed trunk spray soaking the lower 18 inches of trunk and the root flare area down to and slightly below soil level. Repeat in 2–3 weeks. Two applications starting mid-July through late August covers the peak egg-laying window.
  • A dedicated permethrin product (if purchased) can supplement bifenthrin for trunk treatments with slightly better labeled residual on bark.
  • Existing borer infestations (larvae already inside) can be manually removed: scratch away gum and frass, probe tunnels with thin flexible wire to kill larvae.
  • Trees with trunk injury, previous borer attacks, or canker damage are more attractive to egg-laying moths — keep trunks healthy and avoid mechanical damage.
⏱ Critical spray window: mid-July → late August (2 trunk spray applications, 2–3 weeks apart)

Codling Moth

Affects: apple primarily; also pear, plum (minor)
"Wormy apple" — larvae tunnel into fruit, often entering through the calyx end or side, feeding toward the core and leaving a frass-filled tunnel. Reddish-brown entry holes. Fruit may rot around the entry point.
Biology Overwinters as larvae under bark or in ground litter. Adults emerge in May, lay eggs on small fruit and nearby leaves. Two generations in NJ: first generation larvae in fruit June–July, second generation August–September. Once larvae bore into fruit they are protected from insecticides — sprays must target eggs and newly-hatched larvae before entry. Management
  • Your bifenthrin + Sevin cover spray program on apples starting at petal fall provides significant codling moth suppression — these are the correct products and timing.
  • Maintain 10–14 day cover spray intervals through July. Shorten to 7–10 days during second generation (August).
  • Pheromone monitoring traps (delta-shaped sticky traps with codling moth lure) help you confirm presence and time sprays — hang in the upper canopy at pink stage/bloom and check weekly.
  • Remove and destroy all fallen and damaged fruit promptly — larvae exiting fruit pupate nearby and contribute to second generation.
  • Remove loose bark from trunks in winter — larvae overwinter in cocoons under bark.
⏱ Two pressure periods: 1st generation May–July, 2nd generation August–September. Maintain cover sprays through both.

Apple Maggot

Affects: apple primarily; also some stone fruits
Small dimples on fruit surface (egg-laying stings). Internal brown winding tunnels through flesh at harvest. Fruit appears normal externally but is internally ruined. Heavy infestation causes misshapen, russeted fruit.
Biology Adult fly emerges from soil late June–early July in NJ. Females lay eggs just under apple skin; larvae feed inside fruit creating tunnels. One generation per year, but adult flight continues through September. Adults are most active in warm, humid conditions. Management
  • Your bifenthrin cover sprays from late June through harvest provide good suppression. Maintain every 10–14 days.
  • Red sphere sticky traps (available online and at garden centers) are highly effective monitoring and partial control tools — hang 1–2 per tree in late June, coated with Tanglefoot adhesive. They attract and catch adult flies. Replace or re-coat monthly.
  • Do not allow dropped apples to remain on the ground — larvae exit fallen fruit and pupate in the soil.
⏱ Active July through September. Overlap well with your existing cover spray schedule — no special separate timing needed if bifenthrin/Sevin rotation is maintained.

Oriental Fruit Moth

Affects: peach, nectarine primarily; also apple, cherry
Early season: wilting, dying shoot tips ("flagging") — larvae bore into new shoot growth. Late season: larvae enter fruit, often near the stem end, creating internal tunnels with frass. Ooze at entry point.
Biology Multiple generations per year in NJ (3–4). First generation larvae attack shoot tips in May; later generations increasingly target fruit as the season progresses. Adults fly at dusk. Management
  • Your existing bifenthrin + Sevin petal-fall and cover spray program on peaches provides significant OFM suppression alongside its other targets.
  • Prune out wilted shoot tips showing flagging damage in May–June — this removes larvae before they complete development.
  • Increase spray frequency to 7-day intervals on peaches during the 4 weeks before harvest, when later-generation fruit attacks peak.
⏱ Shoot tip damage May–June; fruit damage most serious in July–August. Cover sprays address both generations.

Brown Marmorated Stink Bug (BMSB)

Affects: apple, peach, cherry, plum — puncture and feeding damage to fruit
Corky, spongy brown spots just under the skin of apple (cat-facing). Stone fruit shows surface dimpling and internal breakdown near puncture sites. Distinctive "stink bug" odor when disturbed. Shield-shaped brown insects with banded antennae.
Biology Invasive pest, established throughout NJ. Overwinters in buildings and woodland edges. Adults migrate into orchards in late summer; second generation adults peak August–September and actively feed on maturing fruit. BMSB is a particularly severe pest in NJ given its regional establishment. Management
  • Bifenthrin is one of the most effective home-use insecticides for BMSB. Your August–September cover sprays with bifenthrin on apples and stone fruits directly address BMSB.
  • BMSB tend to attack from orchard edges first — pay extra attention to trees on the perimeter closest to woods or buildings.
  • Exclusion netting over individual trees is highly effective but labor-intensive.
  • No single insecticide provides complete control — combining bifenthrin sprays with removal of overwintering habitat (seal gaps in structures near the orchard) helps reduce populations.
⏱ Peak damage: August–September. Overlap with your SLF/Japanese beetle bifenthrin program — no additional spray timing needed.

Apple Scab — Fruit Infection

Affects: apple (leaves and fruit)
On fruit: olive-green to brown velvety or corky spots on the surface. As fruit grows, lesions become raised and cracked/scabby. Heavily infected fruit is deformed, cracked, and small. Cracks in the fruit skin are open entry points for brown rot (Monilinia) later in the season, causing a compounding disease problem at harvest.
Biology and infection timing Apple scab overwinters in infected fallen leaves. Spores are released during wet spring weather beginning at silver tip/green tip and continuing through June. Young, rapidly expanding tissue (leaves and fruitlets) is most susceptible. Fruit infections from petal fall through June are the most damaging economically — early-infected fruit stays small, cracks as it grows, and frequently drops. Later-season leaf infections continue but are less damaging to the crop. Why this matters for the spray schedule Knowing you have fruit scab confirms that your petal fall through June spray window is the highest-priority period of the entire apple season. A gap in coverage during this stretch — even one missed spray during a wet week — can result in widespread fruit infection. The same protectant fungicides that handle scab (mancozeb, captan, Daconil) also prevent the brown rot secondary infections that follow scab cracks. Management
  • No change in products — mancozeb, captan, myclobutanil, and Daconil are all active against scab. Maintain these in rotation.
  • Shorten spray intervals to 7 days during prolonged wet periods from petal fall through June. Rain washes off protectants — each significant rain event resets your coverage window.
  • Rake and remove fallen leaves in autumn — this is the primary overwintering reservoir for scab inoculum. Destroying leaves reduces next season's infection pressure.
  • Myclobutanil has curative activity up to about 72 hours after an infection event (use it after a missed spray during rain); mancozeb, captan, and Daconil are protectants only.
⏱ Fruit infection window: petal fall → late June (most critical). Leaf infections continue through summer. The petal fall cover spray is the single most important spray for fruit quality on apple.

🫐 Ribes Guide — Currants & Gooseberries

Ribes is the collective term for currants (red, black, white, champagne/pink) and gooseberries. They share similar disease and pest profiles and are managed as a group, with some differences noted below for black currants specifically.

Ribes break dormancy very early — check them now Among the earliest plants in the NJ garden to emerge in spring, Ribes can have leaves beginning to open by mid-March in a warm year. This means your dormant spray window is shorter than for most fruit trees. If you see leaves emerging, switch to the reduced copper rate immediately (1 fl oz/gallon) and time applications to avoid overnight freezes on open foliage.
White Pine Blister Rust — know your surroundings Ribes (especially black currants) are the alternate host for White Pine Blister Rust (Cronartium ribicola), a fungal disease that requires both a five-needled white pine and a Ribes plant to complete its life cycle. It is devastating to white pines. If you have eastern white pines (Pinus strobus) — or neighbors do — within a few hundred feet, be aware of this relationship. Some NJ municipalities have historically restricted or banned black currant cultivation near white pine stands. Copper sprays do not eliminate this risk. This does not mean Ribes cannot be grown in the area; growers should simply be aware of this relationship.

Particularly problematic on soft-fruited Ribes — red currants, white currants, and gooseberries are highly susceptible. Malathion is effective near harvest (check PHI — typically 1 day on soft fruits). Kaolin clay sprays can deter egg-laying. Harvest promptly and frequently — do not leave ripe fruit on the bush. Remove and destroy any soft or oozing fruit immediately.
Currant borer moth
Synanthedon tipuliformis		 Larvae bore into the pith of canes, causing wilting and dieback of individual canes. Cut canes show discolored or hollow pith. Primarily cultural: prune out all affected canes at or below ground level and destroy. Maintaining vigorous new cane growth each year by pruning old wood reduces habitat. No spray is effective once larvae are inside canes.

Black currant vs. red/white/gooseberry differences Black currants are more susceptible to powdery mildew than red or white types — increase myclobutanil frequency on black currants during warm, dry periods. Black currants are also the more significant White Pine Blister Rust alternate host. Gooseberries are highly susceptible to powdery mildew, particularly American varieties — European gooseberry varieties have better resistance. On gooseberries, test wettable sulfur on a small area first before full application; most are tolerant but sensitivity varies by variety.

📋 Key Rules & Safety Guidelines

Never mix copper + lime sulfur These two react to form copper sulfate at concentrations that cause severe phytotoxicity. Always wait at least 2 full weeks between a copper application and a lime sulfur application (and vice versa).
No insecticides during bloom Once flowers are open on any crop, stop all insecticide applications until petal fall. This protects bees and other pollinators. Without pollination, there is no fruit. This applies to Sevin, bifenthrin, and all other insecticides.
Oil + captan / sulfur = phytotoxicity Never apply horticultural or stylet oil within 2 weeks of a captan or sulfur application — in either direction. If you sprayed captan last week, wait another week before using oil, and vice versa.
No sulfur on American grapes — ever American/labrusca-type grapes (Concord, Mars, Diamond, Niagara, Catawba, many French-American hybrids) are susceptible to sulfur phytotoxicity. Wettable sulfur causes interveinal leaf scorch and berry damage on these varieties. Use myclobutanil + horticultural oil instead.
No sulfur on peaches / nectarines Peach and nectarine are sulfur-sensitive stone fruits. Do not apply wettable or dust sulfur during the growing season. Copper is the dormant-season protectant; switch to Daconil or myclobutanil once leaves emerge.
Spray timing, not just products Most protectant fungicides (Daconil, mancozeb, captan, sulfur, copper) must be applied before infection occurs. They do not cure existing disease. Spray on a schedule — do not wait for symptoms to appear. Myclobutanil has some curative ability (up to 72 hours post-infection) but works best as a protectant.
Rotate modes of action Never rely on a single fungicide all season. Rotate between mancozeb, chlorothalonil, myclobutanil, and captan to reduce the risk of resistance developing in fungal populations, particularly for grape black rot and powdery mildew.
Sulfur temperature limit Even on tolerant crops (European grapes), do not apply wettable sulfur when temperatures exceed 85–90°F, or when high temps are forecast within 24–48 hours. Apply early morning or evening in warm weather. High temperatures dramatically increase phytotoxicity risk.
Spray interval in wet weather During prolonged wet periods (multiple rain events per week), shorten spray intervals to every 7 days. Rain washes off contact protectants. Each inch of rain or two weeks of time is a rough guide to reapplication need.
Use NEWA for precise infection-event timing For more accurate spray timing than a fixed calendar, use NEWA (newa.cornell.edu) — Cornell's Network for Environment and Weather Applications. It links to local weather stations and models actual infection risk for apple scab, fire blight, grape diseases, and more based on real temperature and leaf wetness data. A fixed 10–14 day interval is a safe default; NEWA tells you when to tighten that window because conditions are right for infection.
Always read labels — they are the law The label is the legal document governing pesticide use. PHI, application rates, personal protective equipment (PPE), and crop registrations are all specified on the label. When this guide conflicts with the product label, follow the label.
Apple scab cracks invite brown rot — protect fruit from petal fall Scab lesions that crack the fruit skin are entry points for Monilinia brown rot later in the season. An apple that escapes scab damage but gets brown rot at harvest is frustrating; an apple that gets early fruit scab almost always ends up with brown rot too. The petal fall through late June window is where both problems are prevented simultaneously — do not let cover sprays lapse during wet weather in this period.
Apply insecticides in the evening for plum curculio Plum curculio adults are nocturnal — they feed and lay eggs primarily at night, especially when temperatures exceed 70°F. Morning or midday applications are substantially less effective. For petal fall and cover sprays targeting plum curculio on stone fruits and apple, spray at dusk or in the evening hours.
Watch for spider mite flare-up after pyrethroid use Repeated pyrethroid applications (bifenthrin, Sevin) kill predatory mites along with target pests, which can trigger spider mite (two-spotted mite) population explosions in June–July. Symptoms: stippled, bronzed or silvery foliage; fine webbing on leaf undersides; tiny moving specks. Treat immediately with horticultural oil at 2% dilution (not within 2 weeks of captan or sulfur). Do not use pyrethroids for mites — they make the problem worse.
Destroy dropped fruit promptly — all season Fallen fruit harbors plum curculio larvae, codling moth larvae, Oriental fruit moth larvae, and brown rot spores — all of which complete development and amplify next year's pest and disease populations. Pick up and dispose of dropped fruit every 2–3 days. Do not compost.
Destroy diseased material — never compost Mummified fruit, prunings with black knot, infected leaves, and other diseased plant material harbor fungal spores that survive composting. Always bag and send to trash, or burn where permitted. Composting spreads disease.
Ribes dormant window is very short — act in late February Currants and gooseberries break dormancy earlier than almost any other plant in the NJ garden. In warm springs, leaves can begin emerging by mid-March, closing your dormant spray window. Mark late February as the target for dormant copper + oil on all Ribes — do not wait until March. Check plants every few days from late February onward and spray before any leaf tissue shows.
No insecticides during Ribes bloom — fruit set depends on it Currants and gooseberries are highly dependent on bee pollination. Ribes bloom typically occurs in April in NJ — earlier than most tree fruits. Insecticide exposure during bloom directly reduces berry set. Stop all insecticides from the moment flowers open until all petals have fallen. This applies to Sevin, bifenthrin, and malathion.
Spotted Lanternfly is a regulated pest in NJ SLF is subject to NJ state quarantine regulations. Report new infestations to NJ Dept. of Agriculture: 1-833-4BADBUG. Scraping egg masses in late winter is the most effective management action. SLF feeding in August–October can kill or severely damage grape vines — treat it as seriously as any disease.
