01
January
Rest, reflect, and plan the season ahead
DEEP WINTER — Dormancy
ℹ️
January Overview
This is your planning month. No planting yet, but critical groundwork happens on paper. The garden is dormant; garlic sleeps under mulch in Bed 3, berry canes rest in Bed 2.
📋 Planning & Seeds ALL BEDS
  • Order seeds now — popular varieties sell out. Prioritize tomatoes, peppers, eggplant, parsley, dill, cucumbers, lettuce, Swiss chard, carrots.
  • Order gynoecious/parthenocarpic cucumber seeds for Bed 3 trellis (e.g., Diva, Straight Eight, or Marketmore 76). Also consider wilt-resistant varieties — Saladin and Marketmore 76 have some tolerance to bacterial wilt; Diva (parthenocarpic) is also relatively resistant. This matters given the cucumber beetle pressure you're experiencing.
  • Catalog your current seed inventory; note germination dates on packets.
  • Sketch/revise your bed layout; plan crop rotation (don't repeat nightshades in Bed 1 year after year — but you may have limited options with fixed beds).
  • Order any new strawberry plants or garlic varieties now for spring/fall planting.
🔧 Tools & Equipment ALL BEDS
  • Clean, sharpen, and oil all hand tools (hoes, trowels, pruners, loppers).
  • Disinfect pruners and loppers with 70% isopropyl or 10% bleach solution — critical to avoid spreading disease between plants in Bed 2.
  • Inspect and replace drip lines, stakes, cages, or trellises as needed.
  • Bed 1 drip tape: Inspect the tape if you dug it up last fall — check for cracks, splits, or clogged emitters. Hold sections up to a light to spot blocked emitters. Replace any damaged sections now; drip tape is inexpensive and easier to fix on a workbench than in the ground in April.
  • Order grow lights or check lamps for the seed-starting setup.
  • Inventory your chemicals; note expiration dates. Dispose of expired products properly.
👁️ Winter Inspection BED 2 + BED 3 + ROSE BED
  • Bed 3 Garlic: Check mulch cover (should be 4–6" straw/shredded leaves). Add more if thin; heavy freeze without mulch can heave cloves.
  • Bed 2 Berries: After a thaw, inspect dormant canes for scale insects (look for waxy bumps on canes) — treat in February with dormant oil.
  • Look for any cankers or blackened cane tips on raspberries/blackberries — note for pruning in February.
  • Check rose canes for winter dieback; mounding should still be in place.
📚 Learning & Prep ALL BEDS
  • Look into blueberries as a possible Bed 2 addition (they thrive with currants; Holly‑Tone works for both).
  • Consider ordering floating row cover for frost protection in spring/fall.
  • Seed soaking reference — plan ahead: Several seeds in this garden germinate faster and more uniformly with a pre-sow soak. Note which seeds to soak when organizing: Swiss chard (overnight, 8–12 hrs), parsley (24 hrs in warm water), melons and watermelons (4–6 hrs warm water), cucumbers (2–4 hrs optional but helpful), carrots (2–4 hrs — drain and surface-dry before direct sowing so they don't clump), bunching onions (overnight). Tomatoes, peppers, eggplant, and lettuce do not benefit from soaking and may have reduced germination if soaked. Herbs generally do not need soaking.
  • Review last year's notes: what varieties underperformed? Which tasted the best? What disease pressure was highest?
💡 No spraying needed in January. Save the chemicals for when the plants need them. All beds are dormant and exposure is wasted.
02
February
First seeds indoors — peppers and eggplant need a head start
LATE WINTER — Pre-Season Begins
⚠️
Key Timing Window
Start peppers and eggplant indoors Feb 5–15 (10 weeks before last frost, April 15). These are slow germinators and need the lead time. Use a heat mat if possible — they want 80–85°F soil temp to germinate.
🌱 Seed Starting — Indoors BED 1 + POTS
  • Start now (Feb 5–15): All peppers (Bed 1 + pots) and all eggplant. Use sterile seed-starting mix — do NOT use garden soil indoors.
  • Sow 2–3 seeds per cell; thin to 1 after germination. Bottom-water only.
  • Peppers do not benefit from soaking — sow dry. Eggplant also sow dry. Focus heat mat attention on these two; they are the slowest germinators in the garden and need consistent 80–85°F soil temperature more than any other treatment.
  • Provide 14–16 hrs of light with grow lights 2–3" above seedlings.
  • Fertilize weekly with diluted fish emulsion or liquid Plant‑Tone once first true leaves appear.
  • Aim for 65–70°F air temperature during the day; slightly cooler at night.
✂️ Dormant Pruning — Berries BED 2
  • Currants (red, black, white, pink champagne): Remove dead/crossing/weak wood. For black currants: prune out all wood 3+ years old (darker bark). For others: keep best 3–5 canes. Open up the center.
  • Gooseberries: Remove dead and oldest canes; keep 3–4 each of 1-, 2-, and 3-year wood.
  • Black raspberries & blackberries: Remove all dead canes. On blackberries, cut last year's floricanes (canes that fruited) to the ground. Leave vigorous new primocanes.
  • Japanese wineberries: Remove 2-year-old (fruited) canes to ground; tie in new 1-year canes.
  • Disinfect pruners between plants with 70% isopropyl to prevent spreading cane blight.
💊 Dormant Oil Spray BED 2
  • Apply Bonide All Seasons Spray Oil to all berry canes and currant bushes on a mild day (above 40°F) before buds swell — kills overwintering scale, mites, and insect eggs.
  • This is your BEST preventive spray of the year for Bed 2. Don't skip it.
  • Apply at dormant rate (2–4% oil/water solution per label). Coat all canes thoroughly including undersides.
  • Do NOT apply if temps will drop below 32°F within 24 hrs or if buds are already open — risk of damage.
  • Also apply to roses if still dormant (before bud swell).
🌍 Soil Prep & Planning ALL BEDS
  • If not done in fall: pull back mulch from Bed 3 (Bed 3 garlic is waking up under straw — leave 2" of loose straw, don't compact).
  • Test soil pH if possible — NJ soils often trend slightly acidic (6.0–6.5 is ideal for most plants in this zone). Bed 2 currants prefer 5.5–6.5.
03
March
Tomatoes go in the lights; the garden starts waking up
EARLY SPRING — Indoor Season Ramps Up
⚠️
Start Tomatoes March 1–15
That's 6–8 weeks before last frost (April 15). Tomatoes grow faster than peppers — don't start too early or you'll have leggy, pot-bound seedlings by transplant time.
🌱 Seed Starting — Indoors ALL BEDS + POTS
  • Start March 1–10: All tomatoes (Bed 1 + pots, cherry and regular). Sow ¼" deep; germinate at 70–80°F. Thin to 1 per cell.
  • Start March 1–10: Parsley (Pots). Parsley is slow to germinate (2–3 weeks); soak seeds 24 hrs in warm water first — this is one of the seeds where soaking makes a noticeable difference.
  • Start March 1–15: Lettuce (Bed 4 and pots). Use a shallow tray; barely cover seeds. No heat mat needed — lettuce prefers 60–65°F.
  • Start March 10–20: Swiss chard (Bed 4). Soak seeds overnight (8–12 hrs) in warm water — chard seeds have a thick, corky coat that slows germination significantly without soaking. Drain, blot lightly, and sow while damp. Germinate at 65–70°F.
  • Up-pot peppers and eggplant from February into 3–4" pots if they've developed true leaves and roots are showing.
🌍 Bed Soil Prep BEDS 1, 4
  • When soil is workable (not soggy, doesn't clump tightly — mid-to-late March), work 2–3" of compost into Beds 1 and 4.
  • Bed 1: Work in bone meal (phosphorus for roots) plus blood meal (nitrogen) — or substitute Plant‑Tone as a gentler all-in-one organic alternative. Do not combine Plant‑Tone and blood meal; pick one nitrogen source. Till before reinstalling drip tape — complete all soil amendments and tilling first, then lay tape into position.
  • Bed 4: Lighter prep — compost + Plant‑Tone. Lettuce and greens don't need heavy fertilization.
  • Avoid tilling Bed 3 (near woods, wetter) until fully workable — wet soil tilling destroys structure.
💧 Bed 1 — Drip Tape Install BED 1
  • Reinstall drip tape after tilling is complete (late March to mid-April, before transplanting). Lay tape with emitters facing up or at a 45° angle — facing down clogs faster with sediment. Bury 1–2" deep or pin flat on the surface under mulch; burying reduces evaporation and UV degradation.
  • Runtime to deliver 1" of water: Your emitters are 0.25 GPH at 12" spacing = 0.25 gallons per foot of tape per hour. To deliver 1" of water over a 4"-wide raised bed zone, each square foot needs ~0.62 gallons. At 0.25 GPH that's roughly 2.5 hours per irrigation event to deliver a full inch. For tomatoes needing 1–1.5" per week in summer, split across 3 sessions: ~50 minutes per session, 3×/week is a reasonable starting point — adjust based on rainfall and plant response.
  • Soil moisture check: Before trusting the ESP32 schedule blindly, check soil moisture manually 2–3" down 1–2 hours after a run. The soil should be moist but not waterlogged at root depth. Calibrate your runtime against actual conditions early in the season.
  • Flush the lines before burying — run water through to clear any debris from storage. Open the end caps, run for 2–3 minutes, then cap.
  • After burying, run a test cycle and walk the bed looking for any emitters that aren't wetting the soil surface — these may be clogged and need replacement.
⚗️ Bed 1 — Fertigation Options BED 1
  • Cheapest reliable option: Venturi injector (~$15–30, e.g., Mazzei or generic). A Venturi injector installs inline on the supply line — the pressure differential sucks liquid fertilizer from a bucket into the water stream at a fixed ratio (typically 1:100 to 1:200). No power needed, no moving parts, works automatically every time the solenoid opens. This is the most practical fertigation add-on for a drip system. Also consider a peristaltic pump due to low flow rate.
  • What to fertigate with: Use only water-soluble fertilizers — granular organics like Plant‑Tone or blood meal will clog emitters. Best options: Jack's Classic 20-20-20 (early season), Jack's Classic 12-15-30 or Tomato Feed (at flowering/fruiting). These dissolve completely and won't clog 0.25 GPH emitters. Fish emulsion works but can foul lines over time — flush with clean water after every fertigation run.
  • Fertigation schedule suggestion: Fertigate 1×/week during active growth (June–August), plain water on other irrigation days. Reduce to every 2 weeks in September as plants wind down. Always end a fertigation cycle with 5–10 minutes of plain water to flush the tape and prevent salt/residue buildup at the emitters.
  • ESP32 automation tip: Program a "fertigation cycle" in the firmware — same runtime as a normal irrigation event but with the fertigation solenoid pin also pulled HIGH. A simple flag in the schedule (e.g., every 7th day) keeps it clean without manual intervention.
  • Do not fertigate right before heavy rain — same principle as foliar feeding. The fertilizer solution will be diluted and flushed past the root zone before uptake. Check forecasts before scheduling fertigation events.
💊 Fertilize — Garlic BED 3
  • When garlic shoots emerge 2–3" above soil (usually late March in NJ), partially remove the straw mulch — leave a thin layer.
  • Blood meal (high nitrogen): side-dress at 2 lbs per 100 sq ft. Scratch lightly into the surface; water in well. This powers the rapid spring growth phase.
  • Alternatively: diluted liquid fertilizer (fish emulsion 5-1-1) works faster but needs more frequent application.
  • Coffee grounds as a nitrogen supplement: scratch ¼–½ cup of used grounds per plant into the soil around garlic — they add a gentle, slow-release nitrogen boost that complements blood meal or stands in for part of the dose if blood meal is running low. Mix into the top inch; don't leave a thick surface layer (especially with Turkish grind — it crusts).
✂️ Rose Pruning ROSE BED
  • Prune roses when forsythia blooms in Zone 6b — typically late March to early April in Zone 6b. This is nature's signal that the frost window is narrowing.
  • Remove mounding mulch first. Cut out all dead, diseased, or crossing canes. Cut back to outward-facing buds. Remove any canes thinner than a pencil.
  • Hybrid teas: cut back to 12–18" with 3–5 healthy canes. Shrub roses: lighter pruning — remove ~⅓.
  • Dispose of all prunings — do NOT compost rose debris (harbors black spot spores).
💊 First Spray — Roses & Berries BEDS 2 + 3
  • Roses: After pruning, apply first protective fungicide. Use Captain Jack's Copper or Myclobutanil (Immunox) — Immunox is better systemic protection for black spot. Start early; prevention beats cure with roses.
  • Bed 2 berries: If dormant oil was missed in Feb, apply now before buds swell (Bonide All Seasons Oil). Once buds crack open, switch to summer-rate oil or skip to next tool.
  • Apply copper to currants as leaves emerge — prevents anthracnose and leaf spot.
👁️ Early Pest Watch BED 2 + BED 3
  • Aphids often appear early on currant leaves — blast with water or apply insecticidal soap at first sign.
  • Currant sawfly larvae: Small green caterpillars on underside of currant leaves — check weekly once leafed out. Treat with Sevin or insecticidal soap.
  • Strawberries (Bed 2): Do NOT remove winter mulch yet — wait for mid-April. Crowns are still vulnerable to late freezes.
  • Slugs — first emergence: Slugs become active as soon as soil warms above 40°F and spring rains arrive — often March or early April in NJ. This is your first treatment window. Scatter Slug Magic granules (iron phosphate) around Bed 2 strawberries and Bed 3 as soon as soil is workable. Iron phosphate is safe around pets, wildlife, and edibles — it breaks down into iron and phosphate in the soil. Apply in the evening; slugs feed at night. Reapply after rain.
Preen note: You can apply Preen pre‑emergent to Beds 1 and 3 once soil is workable — but only in areas where you are NOT direct sowing seeds. Preen prevents all seed germination, including yours. Wait until after any direct-sow areas are up and established before applying Preen nearby.
04
April
Direct sowing begins; the garden comes alive
SPRING — Soil Activity Begins
⚠️
Last Frost April 15 — Watch Forecasts
Hardened-off cool-season transplants (lettuce, Swiss chard) can go out but cover if temps drop below 28°F. Warm-season crops (tomatoes, peppers, eggplant, cucumbers) stay inside until after May 15.
🌱 Direct Sow Outdoors BEDS 3 + 4
  • Bed 4 (April 1–15): Direct sow bunching onions ¼" deep, rows 6" apart. Pre-soak overnight in cool water before sowing — onion seeds have a short viability window and soaking improves germination rate noticeably. Drain and sow while damp. Also direct-sow lettuce (Bed 4). Soil temps need to be at least 45°F.
  • Bed 3 (April 15+): Direct sow carrots ½" deep. Carrots do NOT transplant well — always direct sow. Optional pre-soak: soak carrot seeds in warm water 2–4 hours before sowing, then drain and spread on a paper towel to surface-dry for 20–30 minutes so they don't clump in the packet. This softens the seed coat and can cut germination time from 2–3 weeks to 10–14 days. Keep soil consistently moist through germination (cover with burlap to retain moisture). Thin to 2–3" spacing once 2" tall.
  • Pots: Direct sow dill — dill does not transplant well. Re-sow every 3–4 weeks for continuous supply.
🥒 Start Cucumbers Indoors BED 3 + POTS
  • Start cucumbers indoors April 15–20 (transplant outdoors after May 15 — only 3–4 weeks ahead). Starting indoors is the right call — direct-sown seedlings are extremely vulnerable to cucumber beetle feeding before they establish.
  • Cucumbers hate root disturbance — use biodegradable peat pots or cells; transplant pot and all.
  • Optional seed soak: Soaking cucumber seeds in warm water for 2–4 hours before sowing speeds germination, especially in cooler conditions. Drain and sow while damp. Unlike parsley and chard where soaking is strongly recommended, cucumbers germinate reasonably well without it — but it doesn't hurt.
  • Germinate at 70–85°F. They grow fast — don't start too early or they'll be overgrown and stressed at transplant.
  • Beetle-proofing strategy: Have floating row cover ready at transplant time. Covering transplants immediately with row cover is the single most effective way to prevent cucumber beetle feeding — and since your parthenocarpic/gynoecious varieties don't need pollination, you can keep the cover on much longer (remove only once plants are large and established, or remove briefly at flowering if any open-pollinated varieties are included).
  • Start Watermelon and Melon seeds at the same time as cucumbers, soaking them the same way before planting.
🌿 Transplant — Cool Season Crops BED 4 + POTS
  • After hardening off (1–2 weeks of outdoor exposure, increasing daily), transplant lettuce and Swiss chard to Bed 4 (early-to-mid April). They tolerate light frost (28–32°F).
  • Move parsley outdoors to pots — it handles light frost.
  • Space lettuce 8–10" apart (or 6" for baby leaf harvest). Swiss chard 6–9" apart.
  • Keep a frost cloth or old bedsheet handy for late freezes.
🌱 Fertilize — Berries & Strawberries BED 2
  • Mid-April: Remove winter straw mulch from strawberry crowns. Rake off gently — don't disturb roots.
  • Apply Plant‑Tone (5-3-3) around all strawberry plants. Work lightly into soil; water in well. Plant‑Tone is the preferred organic option for strawberries.
  • Currants, gooseberries, wineberries, raspberries, blackberries: Apply Holly‑Tone (4-3-4) around drip line of each plant — these all prefer slightly acidic soil (5.5–6.5 pH). Holly‑Tone encourages that.
  • Do NOT over-fertilize nitrogen on berries — you'll get lush leaves but poor fruit set.
💊 Spray Schedule — April BED 2 + ROSE BED
  • Currants & Gooseberries: Apply copper (Captain Jack's Copper) as leaves emerge — excellent prevention of anthracnose, white pine blister rust (if white pines are nearby), and leaf spot. Reapply after heavy rain.
  • Roses: Begin biweekly Myclobutanil (Immunox) spray — best systemic protection for black spot and powdery mildew in roses. Superior to copper or Daconil for roses. Start preventively.
  • All Bed 2 canes: Insecticidal soap for early aphid colonies forming on new growth.
🌸 Peonies & Perennials BED 3
  • Remove any remaining winter mulch from peony crowns — expose the red "eyes" (emerging shoots). Do NOT cover the shoots; peonies need cold exposure to bloom.
  • Bone meal: apply a light ring around each peony — phosphorus supports flower development.
  • Perennial flowers: pull back dead stems/mulch, let emerging growth breathe.
  • Keep an eye on garlic: shoots should be 4–6" now. Apply a second nitrogen side-dressing (blood meal or Plant‑Tone) if growth seems slow.
05
May
The big transplant month — garden goes full throttle after May 15
SPRING → EARLY SUMMER — Last Frost Behind Us
✅
After May 15: Transplant Warm-Season Crops
Tomatoes, peppers, eggplant, cucumbers, and all tender herbs go out after the last frost date. Harden off for 7–10 days before planting (set outside in shade, gradually increase sun and time outdoors).
🍅 Transplant — Warm Season BED 1 + POTS
  • After May 15: Transplant tomatoes (Bed 1 and pots) — bury deeply (up to 2/3 of the stem; roots form on buried stem). Space 18–24" for determinate, 24–36" for indeterminate.
  • Transplant peppers (Bed 1 and pots) and eggplant (Bed 1). Space 18" apart. Add a stake at planting time to avoid disturbing roots later.
  • Transplant cucumbers (Bed 3 trellis and pots) — gently, do not disturb roots. Space 12" on trellis. Immediately cover with floating row cover after planting — don't wait to see beetles first. Cucumber beetles find plants within hours of transplanting and the bacterial wilt they carry has no cure.
  • Set out rosemary, thyme, mint (pot separately — mint is invasive), and remaining parsley.
  • Melon & watermelon transplants (after May 15 — soil temp 65°F minimum, 70°F+ preferred): Transplant into the melon patch in full sun. Space cantaloupes 18–24" apart in rows at least 4–5 ft apart; watermelons 24–36" with 6 ft between rows. For small-space growing, bush watermelon varieties (Sugar Baby, Bush Sugar Baby) and small cantaloupes can be trellised vertically — support developing fruits in mesh slings tied to the trellis. Plant on slight mounds for drainage. Cover immediately with floating row cover — melons are targeted by the same cucumber beetles that devastate cucumbers. Remove row cover once female flowers open (flowers with a tiny fruit at the base) since melons require bee pollination to set fruit.
  • Black plastic mulch for melons: Laying black plastic mulch over the melon patch before transplanting significantly warms soil, conserves moisture, and suppresses weeds. It can add 1–2 weeks of effective growing season in Zone 6b — genuinely useful for marginal-length varieties. Transplant through slits cut in the plastic.
  • Water well at transplant with diluted fish emulsion or Plant‑Tone liquid to reduce transplant shock.
🌱 Fertilize at Transplant BED 1 + POTS
  • Bed 1 (pre-planting if not done in April): Plant‑Tone or 10-10-10 worked into soil at label rates. Plant‑Tone preferred (organic, slow‑release, less risk of burn). Use 10-10-10 sparingly — fast‑release fertilizers can stress transplants.
  • Bone meal in the planting hole: add a tablespoon at the bottom of each tomato/pepper/eggplant hole — phosphorus supports root establishment.
  • For pots: use a quality potting mix, then work Osmocote 14-14-14 into the top inch at planting — this is the best slow-release base for containers (feeds ~3 months). Top-dress with Plant‑Tone monthly and supplement with liquid feed every 10–14 days through the season. See June fertilizer notes for full pot strategy.
  • Melon Patch — at transplant: Work compost and Plant‑Tone into the soil before planting. Add bone meal to each planting hole for root establishment. Melons are heavy feeders — similar nutritional demands to tomatoes. Avoid over-applying nitrogen early; focus on phosphorus and balanced feeding. Once vines are running (June), switch to a lower-nitrogen feed to encourage fruit set over leaf growth.
  • Garlic (Bed 3): Last nitrogen boost — blood meal or liquid fertilizer. After mid-May, reduce nitrogen or you'll push leaves at the expense of bulb development.
✂️ Training & Pruning BED 1 + BED 2
  • Tomatoes (indeterminate): Begin suckering — remove side shoots that emerge in the crotch between stem and branch. Single-stem or 2-stem training for caged/staked indeterminate types. Determinate types need less suckering.
  • Cucumbers (Bed 3): Begin training vines onto trellis. Tie gently with soft twine at regular intervals as they grow.
  • Melons & watermelons — vine training: Once row cover is removed at flower set, begin training vines. For trellised varieties, guide the main vine upward and tie loosely every 12". Pinch lateral shoots beyond the first 2–3 female flowers on each lateral to concentrate energy into fruit. On the ground, train vines in the same direction to reduce foot traffic damage. Place a piece of tile, wood, or straw under developing fruits to prevent rot from soil contact.
  • Black raspberries (Bed 2): When primocanes (new shoots) reach 3.5–4 ft, pinch (tip) the top 3–4". This forces laterals to develop — dramatically increases next year's yield.
  • Strawberries (Bed 2): Remove runners (runners divert energy from fruit; allow just 2–3 runners to root per plant if you want to expand the bed).
💊 Spray Schedule — May BED 2 + ROSE BED
  • Roses: Continue biweekly Myclobutanil (Immunox). Add an insecticide if aphids are present — insecticidal soap first (gentler), or bifenthrin if heavy infestation.
  • Strawberries (Bed 2): Before bloom: apply copper for gray mold (Botrytis) prevention — critical in wet springs. Do NOT use Daconil/chlorothalonil on strawberries near harvest — long PHI (pre‑harvest interval).
  • Currants/Gooseberries: Continue copper spray every 10–14 days or after rain. Watch for aphid colonies — apply insecticidal soap as needed.
  • Bed 1 (tomatoes etc.): Begin prophylactic copper spray biweekly — early blight prevention starts now, not after you see symptoms.
  • 🪲 Cucumber beetles (Bed 3 + Pots) — START NOW: Before placing row cover, spray soil surface and base of transplants with bifenthrin 7.9% to kill any beetles already at ground level. If row cover is not in place, begin foliar bifenthrin sprays every 7 days immediately — do not wait for visual confirmation. By the time you see beetles, wilt inoculation may have already occurred.
👁️ Pest Watch — May ALL BEDS
  • Flea beetles: Tiny jumping beetles that pepper holes in eggplant and pepper leaves. Apply insecticidal soap or neem oil — or use floating row cover on eggplant until established. Kaolin clay is also very effective — worth adding if not already on hand.
  • Strawberry blossom weevil (clipper): Tiny snout beetle that clips flower buds. Inspect daily at bloom; use Sevin or bifenthrin at first sign — timing is critical (before eggs are laid in buds).
  • Currant sawfly larvae: Inspect undersides of currant leaves. Green caterpillars defoliate quickly. Spray insecticidal soap or Sevin if present.
  • Set up Japanese beetle traps AWAY from the garden (they attract more beetles to an area — don't put traps in the garden).
  • Slugs — peak spring activity: May is often your worst slug month — cool nights, frequent rain, and tender new transplants everywhere. Seedlings and young transplants of tomatoes, peppers, eggplant, and lettuce are extremely vulnerable; a single night of slug feeding can destroy a transplant. Apply Slug Magic granules in a ring around each bed edge and around individual transplants in Beds 1, 2, and 3 (especially Bed 3 near woods — chronically worse). Reapply every 7–14 days and after any significant rain. Also scatter around strawberry plants — slugs hide under the leaves and feed on developing fruit from below, leaving ragged holes that are easy to miss until the damage is done.
  • 🪲 Cucumber beetles — melons equally vulnerable: Cucumber beetles attack melons just as aggressively as cucumbers and transmit the same bacterial wilt. Keep row cover on melon transplants until female flowers open. If beetles are found before cover goes on, spray the soil surface and plant bases with bifenthrin.
  • 🪲 Cucumber beetles — inspect at transplant: Check the soil around cucumber transplants for striped (3 black stripes) and spotted (12 black dots) beetles within the first few days of planting. They find new transplants within hours. If row cover is not in place and you see even one beetle, spray bifenthrin immediately — a single striped beetle feeding is enough to transmit bacterial wilt. This pest caused your previous plant losses and deserves zero tolerance.
🌱 Bed Maintenance ALL BEDS
  • Apply Preen pre‑emergent around transplants in Beds 1, 3, 4 (not over direct-sown areas still germinating). Apply when soil is moist; water in lightly. Do NOT apply near strawberries or any areas where runners will root.
  • Mulch all beds (2–3" wood chips or straw) once transplants are in — reduces weeds, retains moisture, moderates soil temp. Critical for tomatoes (prevents soil splash-up that spreads blight).
  • Begin consistent watering schedule — tomatoes, peppers, and eggplant want even moisture. Inconsistent watering causes blossom end rot and blossom drop.
  • Install tomato cages or stake-and-weave system before plants get too large.
06
June
First harvests arrive; spray vigilance peaks with humidity
EARLY SUMMER — Growth Explosion
🪲
Japanese Beetles Arrive Late June
They emerge around June 20–July 10 in NJ and will hit your roses, raspberries, blackberries, wineberries, and even peppers hard. Have your spray ready. They're most active mid-morning on warm sunny days.
🍓 First Harvests BEDS 2 + 4 + POTS
  • Strawberries (Bed 2): Peak harvest June 1–20. Pick when fully red — don't let overripe fruit sit (attracts spotted wing drosophila and invites disease). Check daily at peak.
  • Lettuce & Swiss chard (Bed 4 + Pots): Begin cut-and-come-again harvest. Cut outer leaves; leave inner growth.
  • Herbs (Pots): Parsley, thyme, chives — harvest regularly to encourage bushy growth. Don't let dill flower unless you want seeds.
  • Bed 4 bunching onions: Harvest when tops are 8–10" and base is ¼–½" diameter.
  • Peonies: Cut blooms in bud stage for longest vase life. Deadhead spent flowers immediately — prevents botrytis (gray mold).
🌱 Fertilize — June ALL BEDS
  • Bed 1 — Tomatoes: Once first flowers appear, switch to a lower-nitrogen fertilizer. High nitrogen now pushes leaves at the expense of fruit. Use Plant‑Tone bimonthly OR a tomato-specific fertilizer (3-4-6 ratio preferred at flowering).
  • Bed 1 — Peppers & Eggplant: Plant‑Tone monthly. Both are moderate feeders.
  • Pots — fertilizing strategy: Containers leach nutrients with every watering, so they need more attention than in-ground beds. Use a three-layer approach: (1) Osmocote 14-14-14 (slow-release granules, ~3 months) worked into the top inch of potting mix at planting — this is your baseline feed and the most efficient option for containers; (2) a monthly top-dress of Plant‑Tone scratched into the surface for organic supplement; (3) liquid fish emulsion or water-soluble fertilizer every 10–14 days as your main active feed. Blood meal is not recommended for pots — it leaches out almost immediately and containers lack the microbial activity needed to break down organics effectively. Rain timing: avoid liquid fertilizing within 24 hours of forecast heavy rain (over ½ inch) — it will flush through before roots can absorb it. Light rain after fertilizing is fine and actually helps water it in. Best practice: apply on a dry morning with no heavy rain forecast for at least a day.
  • Bed 3 — Garlic: Stop all fertilizing by June 1. Focus shifts to bulb development; excess nitrogen now creates loose, poorly wrapped bulbs.
  • Roses: Apply Plant‑Tone or a rose-specific fertilizer around the drip line after first flush of bloom. Water in well.
💊 Spray Schedule — June ALL BEDS
  • Bed 1 — Tomatoes/Eggplant/Peppers: Biweekly copper (Captain Jack's Copper) OR rotate with Mancozeb for early blight/septoria prevention. Spray in the evening — copper phytotoxicity risk in heat of day. Mancozeb has no PHI concerns at this stage.
  • Bed 3 — Cucumbers: Begin copper or neem oil spray to prevent downy mildew and angular leaf spot. Biweekly.
  • Melon Patch: Begin the same copper/neem biweekly rotation used for cucumbers — melons are susceptible to the same fungal diseases (downy mildew, powdery mildew, angular leaf spot). Spray in evening. Once fruits are sizing up, observe all label pre-harvest intervals.
  • 🪲 Cucumber beetles (Bed 3 + Pots) — ACTIVE SEASON BEGINS: If row cover has been removed, begin bifenthrin 7.9% foliar sprays every 7 days — this is non-negotiable given how quickly cucumber beetles spread bacterial wilt. Apply in early morning when beetles are sluggish and less likely to flee before spray contact. Focus on the base of plants, undersides of lower leaves, and soil surface around stems. If row cover is still on, leave it — every day under cover is a day without wilt exposure.
  • Roses: Continue biweekly Myclobutanil (Immunox). In late June, add a pyrethroid (bifenthrin or Sevin) for Japanese beetle control. Spray in early morning.
  • Bed 2 Raspberries/Blackberries: Sevin or bifenthrin for Japanese beetles at first sighting. Apply to foliage; avoid spraying open flowers (bee harm).
  • Strawberries (Bed 2 — post-harvest): After the last pick, apply copper for crown rot and leaf spot. Also neem oil for spider mites if warm/dry spell.
✂️ Training & Pruning BEDS 1 + 2 + 3
  • Tomatoes (Bed 1 + Pots): Continue weekly suckering of indeterminate types. Train to stakes/cages. Remove lower leaves touching the soil (prevents soil-splash blight transmission).
  • Cucumbers (Bed 3 + Pots): Continue tying to trellis. Remove any yellow or diseased leaves promptly. If row cover is still on, check underneath weekly — once plants are vigorous and past the vulnerable seedling stage (4–6 true leaves, actively climbing) you can remove it if beetle pressure seems low, or leave it on entirely if varieties are parthenocarpic.
  • Black raspberries (Bed 2): If you missed tipping in May, tip now (any canes under 3.5–4 ft that haven't been tipped yet).
  • Roses: Deadhead spent blooms to encourage rebloom on remontant varieties. Cut back to a 5-leaflet leaf with an outward-facing bud.
  • Thin lettuce (Bed 4) if direct-sown areas are crowded — thin to 6–8".
👁️ Pest & Disease Watch ALL BEDS
  • Slugs on strawberries (Bed 2) — CRITICAL: Peak strawberry slug damage coincides with ripening fruit in June. Slugs rasp irregular holes into ripe and near-ripe berries from the underside — the damage is often hidden under the fruit and looks like rot at first glance. Inspect fruit daily by lifting and turning it. Maintain an active ring of Slug Magic granules around the entire strawberry bed edge and between rows. Reduce mulch depth temporarily if slug pressure is high — dense straw is prime daytime slug shelter. Also check under any boards, pots, or debris near Bed 2 and remove them. Beer traps (shallow dish of cheap beer sunk to soil level) are a useful supplement — slugs are attracted to the yeast, fall in, and drown. Check and refill every 2–3 days.
  • Tomato hornworms: Large green caterpillars — hand-pick; they're surprisingly hard to spot. Check weekly; look for defoliated branches and dark frass (droppings).
  • Aphids on pepper tips: Spray with insecticidal soap or strong water jet.
  • Yellow spots on cucumber leaves — know the difference: Angular leaf spot produces angular yellow patches bounded by leaf veins, with a water-soaked look — caused by bacteria, spread by cucumber beetles and splashing water. Downy mildew produces larger irregular yellow patches on the upper surface with gray-purple fuzzy growth on the underside. Both spread fast. Copper spray addresses both; improve airflow by training vertically. Downy mildew: also use Myclobutanil. Angular leaf spot: copper is your best tool — no cure once established, remove affected leaves immediately.
  • Powdery mildew on cucumbers: White-gray powdery coating (different from the yellow spots above — this sits on top of leaves). Apply neem oil or Myclobutanil. Improve airflow if dense planting.
  • Peonies — Botrytis: Gray mold on buds or petals — remove infected plant material immediately. Apply copper spray. Ensure good airflow.
  • Carrot rust fly (Bed 3): Yellowing tops, tunneled roots — apply row cover over carrot bed to prevent egg laying; difficult to spray once established.
  • Scattered Preen touch-up in high-weed areas of beds.
💧 Watering & Mulch ALL BEDS
  • Tomatoes need 1–2" of water per week. Deep, infrequent watering (at soil level, not overhead) is far superior to shallow daily watering. Overhead watering promotes fungal disease.
  • Replenish mulch if it has broken down or been displaced. Aim for 2–3" consistently — especially in Bed 1 around tomatoes.
  • Begin succession sowing of lettuce and dill every 3 weeks (as summer heat bolts older plantings).
  • Coffee grounds — ongoing compost addition: Keep adding daily grounds to your compost pile through the season. In summer heat, a compost pile with regular coffee ground additions (balanced with dry brown material — dried leaves, cardboard) can reach 130–160°F and break down in 6–8 weeks. Turn the pile weekly for fastest results. Avoid adding grounds directly around acid-sensitive plants mid-season as a soil drench — at this time of year the nitrogen benefit is modest and the crusting risk in dry weather is real.

🍅
Late Blight Alert — Act Preventively
Late blight (Phytophthora infestans) devastates tomatoes rapidly in NJ's humid July nights. Once you see greasy dark spots on leaves, it spreads fast. Your best tools: Mancozeb and copper on a weekly schedule during wet periods.
🍇 Peak Harvest ALL BEDS
  • Tomatoes (Bed 1 + Pots): Cherry tomatoes ripen first (July 1–15 often). Regular tomatoes typically July 15–August. Check daily — don't let over-ripe fruit attract pests.
  • Peppers (Bed 1 + Pots): Green peppers ready at full size. Let ripen to red/yellow/orange for sweetness and higher vitamin C.
  • Eggplant (Bed 1): Harvest when skin is glossy and slightly soft to the touch. Don't let them over-ripen (seeds become bitter).
  • Melons — harvest timing (critical to get right): Unlike tomatoes, melons do NOT continue to ripen or sweeten after harvest — they must be picked at peak ripeness on the vine. Cantaloupe/muskmelon: "Full slip" is the gold standard — press gently at the stem attachment; a ripe melon detaches with almost no resistance and leaves a clean circular scar. Also look for: fully developed tan netting, strong sweet fragrance at the blossom end, and a color change from green to gold-tan between the netting. A slight give at the blossom end is acceptable; a soft blossom end with fermenting smell means overripe. Watermelon: The tendril closest to the fruit dries and turns brown when ripe. The ground spot (underside where fruit rests) turns from white to creamy yellow. The thump test (hollow vs. dull thud) requires experience — use it alongside the tendril test, not alone. Do not rely on size alone — check multiple indicators.
  • Cucumbers (Bed 3 + Pots): Check DAILY — they grow incredibly fast. Overgrown cucumbers reduce the plant's productivity. Parthenocarpic types will set without pollination.
  • Currants (Bed 2): Black currants first (late June/early July), then red/white/pink champagne (July 1–20). Harvest when fully colored and slightly soft. Taste test — they should be sweet-tart.
  • Black raspberries (Bed 2): July harvest — they separate easily from the core when ripe (unlike blackberries).
  • Wineberries (Bed 2): Mid-to-late July. Visually striking orange-red; sweet/tart. Pick when fully colored.
  • Garlic (Bed 3): Harvest when 5–6 of the lower leaves turn brown (typically mid-July in NJ). Cure in a warm, shaded, ventilated area for 3–4 weeks.
  • Swiss chard (Bed 4): Continue outer-leaf harvest.
💊 Spray Schedule — July ALL BEDS
  • Bed 1 — Tomatoes (CRITICAL): Increase to weekly spray during warm/humid periods. Rotate: Mancozeb (week 1) → Captain Jack's Copper (week 2). Mancozeb is the superior contact fungicide for late blight. Note: Mancozeb has a 5-day pre‑harvest interval — plan accordingly.
  • Bed 3 — Cucumbers & Melon Patch: Weekly neem oil or copper for powdery mildew. If severe: Myclobutanil (Immunox) is the most effective systemic option for cucurbits — read label for rates.
  • 🪲 Cucumber beetles (Bed 3 + Pots) — PEAK PRESSURE: July is peak cucumber beetle season in NJ. Maintain bifenthrin 7.9% every 7 days without fail if plants are uncovered. Do NOT extend the spray interval during hot humid spells — beetle populations are at maximum and bacterial wilt transmission risk is highest. If a plant suddenly wilts despite adequate water, perform the stem-pull test immediately (cut stem, pull apart slowly — white stringy threads = bacterial wilt). Remove and bag wilted plants the same day; do not compost.
  • Japanese beetles (Beds 2 + 3): Bifenthrin 7.9% is your most effective and persistent option. Sevin (lambda-cyhalothrin formulation) is also effective. Apply early morning when beetles are sluggish. Reapply every 7–10 days as long as beetles are present.
  • Roses: Continue biweekly Immunox + add bifenthrin for beetles if not already included.
  • STOP spraying berries (Bed 2) during active fruit harvest — observe label pre‑harvest intervals. Copper: 0 days PHI. Sevin: check label (varies by formulation). Bifenthrin: 1–3 days PHI. Insecticidal soap: 0 days PHI.
🌱 Fertilize — July BEDS 1 + POTS
  • Melons at fruit set — fertilizer shift: Once fruits are marble-size and sizing rapidly, reduce nitrogen and increase potassium. A fertilizer with a ratio like 5-10-15 or similar (low N, moderate P, high K) supports fruit sweetness and size. Consistent watering is critical at this stage — irregular moisture causes cracking, bland flavor, and blossom end rot. Reduce watering slightly as fruits approach full maturity (2 weeks before expected harvest) to concentrate sugars.
  • Tomatoes showing blossom end rot? This is calcium deficiency compounded by irregular watering — NOT a soil calcium shortage in most NJ soils. Solution: consistent deep watering. Ground eggshells are too slow to help mid-season. A foliar calcium spray (calcium chloride solution) works faster if needed.
  • Pots (ALL): Continue liquid feed every 10–14 days — this is critical for potted plants in July heat. They're at peak growth and leach nutrients rapidly.
  • Bed 1 tomatoes: If you're fertigating, a weekly dose of Jack's 12-15-30 or Tomato Feed through the drip tape replaces mid-season side-dressing. If not yet fertigating, surface-apply Plant‑Tone or 10-10-10 lightly (1 cup per plant) mid-July and water in manually — the buried tape won't distribute surface-applied granules effectively on its own.
✂️ Post-Harvest Cane Management BED 2
  • Wineberries: After harvest, remove the fruited (2nd-year) canes to the ground. Leave the vigorous new green primocanes — these will fruit next year. Tie new canes to supports.
  • Black raspberries: Remove all floricanes (fruited canes — they're woody and have short brown stubs). Leave the green primocanes you tipped in May/June.
  • Currants/Gooseberries: Remove any exhausted old canes (4+ years old) now if missed in February. Thin crowded centers.
👁️ Pest & Disease Watch ALL BEDS
  • Spider mites (Bed 1 + Pots): Stippled leaves, fine webbing on underside in hot/dry weather. Apply insecticidal soap or neem oil — get undersides of leaves. Bonide All Seasons Oil at summer rate also works. Mites develop resistance to pyrethroids — don't use bifenthrin for mites.
  • Slugs in summer: Activity drops during hot dry weather but surges again during rainy stretches or after irrigation. After any period of several rainy days, do an evening inspection with a flashlight — go out 1–2 hours after dark when slugs are actively feeding. Reapply Slug Magic granules after heavy rain (iron phosphate dissolves slowly but heavy rain speeds breakdown). Keep granules active around tomato plant bases — slugs target lower fruit and will climb stems at night.
  • Spotted wing drosophila (Bed 2): Small fly that lays eggs in ripening fruit. Pick fruit promptly (daily). Refrigerate harvested fruit immediately. Spinosad (Captain Jack's Deadbug — worth buying if not already on hand) is the best control.
  • Bacterial wilt — your #1 cucumber killer: Caused by the bacterium Erwinia tracheiphila, spread exclusively by striped and spotted cucumber beetles feeding on plants. Symptoms: one or more vines wilt suddenly and completely despite adequate water, then the whole plant collapses and dies within days. There is no cure — once wilting appears, the plant is lost. Diagnostic test: cut a wilted stem and slowly pull the two ends apart — if you see thin white stringy threads bridging the gap, it's bacterial wilt. Remove and bag the plant immediately (don't compost). Prevention is everything: row cover from day one, plus bifenthrin or Sevin sprayed on the soil and base of plants before cover goes on. If you remove the cover, spray foliage with bifenthrin every 7 days during peak beetle season (June–August).
  • Check rose hips: if roses are setting hips instead of reblooming, deadhead more aggressively.
🌿 Herb Maintenance (Pots) POTS
  • Mint: Harvest aggressively to prevent flowering. Once mint flowers, flavor declines.
  • Basil (if applicable): Pinch flowers constantly.
  • Dill: Allow one plant to flower and go to seed if you want dill seed for pickling. Otherwise deadhead to extend leaf production.
  • Rosemary: Drought-tolerant — don't overwater in summer heat. Let dry between waterings.
  • Pot lettuce: likely bolting in July heat. Replace with new starts or wait until late August for fall planting.

LATE SUMMER — Transition Begins
🫐 Harvest Continues ALL BEDS
  • Blackberries (Bed 2): Late July through August. Pick when fully black AND soft — if they're still firm, wait another day or two. They should separate easily with no resistance.
  • Tomatoes, peppers, eggplant, cucumbers: Peak production. Don't let anything over-ripen on the vine — that signals the plant to stop producing.
  • Peppers: Harvest at green if you need them; leaving on vine to ripen red takes another 2–3 weeks but dramatically improves flavor and nutrition.
  • Swiss chard (Bed 4): Can take light frost — will produce into October if not cut to the ground.
  • Melons & watermelons — final harvest push: All melons must be harvested before first frost (Oct 15). With a Sept 1–Oct 1 ripening window for most Zone 6b varieties, late August is critical — check all developing fruits every other day. If a cold forecast threatens before fruits are ripe, cut the vine a few inches from the melon and bring indoors — cantaloupes will continue to soften (though not sweeten) off the vine for a few days. Watermelons will not improve off the vine at all. For very late-season plants, removing all but the 2–3 largest fruits now forces the plant to ripen what it has rather than starting new ones that won't finish.
🌱 Fall Planting Starts — Indoors & Direct BED 4 + POTS

✂️ Strawberry Bed Renovation BED 2
  • Renovate strawberry bed within 2 weeks of last harvest (by late July or early August). This allows the plant maximum time to rebuild before winter.
  • Mow or cut foliage to 1–2" above the crown. Thin plants to 6–8" apart, removing old or weakly-rooted plants. Till narrow rows between plant rows.
  • Apply Plant‑Tone or 10-10-10 after renovation and water in. This critical feed sets up next year's crop.
  • Apply copper spray after renovation to reduce leaf spot and crown rot going into fall.
  • Allow runners selected for bed expansion to root in place.
💊 Spray Schedule — August BEDS 1 + 3
  • Bed 1 — Tomatoes (CRITICAL): Late blight is most destructive in August warm, humid nights. Maintain weekly fungicide rotation: Mancozeb alternating with Captain Jack's Copper. If late blight is confirmed (look for olive-colored lesions on leaves, white fuzzy growth on leaf undersides), apply Mancozeb immediately — it's your best contact tool. Observe 5-day pre‑harvest interval.
  • Bed 3 — Cucumbers & Melon Patch: Powdery mildew is almost universal in August. Neem oil biweekly as a minimum. Myclobutanil (Immunox) if the infestation is ahead of you — systemic action works from inside the plant.
  • 🪲 Cucumber beetles (Bed 3 + Pots) — SECOND WAVE: A second generation of cucumber beetles hatches in August. Continue bifenthrin 7.9% every 7–10 days. If plants have survived to this point without wilt, they are more resistant to sudden collapse — but beetles will still cause feeding damage and spread disease to any remaining healthy vines. Maintain spray schedule through end of harvest.
  • Roses: Continue biweekly Immunox. Japanese beetles should be winding down in late August — reduce pyrethroid sprays accordingly.
  • Blackberries (Bed 2 — after harvest): Once harvest is complete, apply copper spray to prevent cane blight and rust.
👁️ Pest Watch — August ALL BEDS
  • Cucumber beetles (striped and spotted): Second wave intensifies in August. Striped beetle (yellow with 3 black stripes) is the primary bacterial wilt vector — even a few feeding punctures can infect a plant. Spotted beetle (yellow with 12 black spots) also transmits wilt but less efficiently. Both chew ragged holes in leaves and flowers. Use bifenthrin every 7–10 days on foliage; apply in early morning. If plants were lost earlier in the season to sudden wilt, cucumber beetles are established in the area — row cover from day one next year is essential.
  • Hornworms: Second-generation tomato hornworms appear. Look for defoliated branches and large green caterpillars. Hand-pick or spray Sevin for heavy infestations.
  • Pepper maggot fly: Causes pitted, prematurely red peppers. Bifenthrin as a preventive spray in early August.
  • Blackberry leaf spot: Yellow-orange spots, early defoliation on blackberries. Apply copper after harvest.
🌱 Fertilize — August BED 2 + ROSE BED
  • Bed 2 berries (post-harvest): After all fruiting is done, apply a final Holly‑Tone feeding. This fall feed supports root development and cane hardening before winter. Do not use high-nitrogen fertilizers this late — you don't want soft new growth that won't harden before frost.
  • Roses: Stop heavy fertilizing after Aug 15 — you want new growth to harden off, not push soft shoots into fall. A light Plant‑Tone is fine through Aug 1; then stop.
  • Bed 1 pots: Continue liquid feeding through August — containers need it. Reduce frequency after Sept 1.

EARLY FALL — Wind-Down & Plant
🧄 Plant Garlic — Bed 3 BED 3
  • Plant garlic Sept 15 – Oct 10 in NJ (after first light frost but while soil is still workable — typically late Sept/early Oct in Zone 6b).
  • Plant hardneck varieties (Rocambole, Porcelain, Purple Stripe, Music) for NJ's climate — they're better suited to cold winters and have superior flavor.
  • Plant cloves 2" deep, 6" apart, pointed end up. Add bone meal at bottom of each hole.
  • Bed 3 being wetter/shadier: ensure garlic placement has best drainage within that bed. Garlic rots in waterlogged soil. Consider raised rows if drainage is problematic.
  • Mulch after planting with 2–3" of straw (increase to 4–6" after hard freeze in November).
🥬 Fall Harvests BEDS 1 + 4 + POTS
  • Fall lettuce and Swiss chard (Bed 4): Begin harvesting as outer leaves reach size. Swiss chard improves after light frost.
  • Melons & watermelons: All remaining fruits must be harvested before first frost (Oct 15). Don't leave any on the vine past the first light frost warning — cover with old bedsheets if a surprise frost threatens and fruits are still ripening. After final harvest, pull vines immediately and dispose of (do not compost if powdery mildew or disease was present). Let the melon patch soil rest; add compost and till lightly.
  • Tomatoes (Bed 1 + Pots): Harvest all mature fruit before expected frost. Green tomatoes can ripen indoors at room temperature (NOT in the refrigerator — cold ruins flavor).
  • Peppers: Harvest all before first frost — they don't survive freezing. Can roast and freeze in quantity for winter use.
  • Eggplant: Same as peppers — harvest before frost.
  • Carrots (Bed 3): Can be harvested through October. A light frost actually sweetens them. Dig before hard ground freezes.
✂️ Berry Cane Management BED 2
  • Blackberries: Remove any remaining fruited floricanes that weren't cut after harvest. Tie in new primocanes for winter support.
  • Raspberries/Wineberries: Ensure all fruited canes are removed. New canes should be secured to trellis or wire.
  • Slugs — fall resurgence: September brings cooler temps and autumn rains — slug activity surges again, often as bad as spring. This is your second critical treatment window. Fall lettuce, Swiss chard, and newly planted garlic are all vulnerable. Apply Slug Magic granules across all active beds, especially Bed 3 (garlic just planted) and Bed 4 (fall greens). Maintain through October until first hard frost. Also a good time to do an evening flashlight inspection to gauge the local slug population — knowing how bad it is helps you calibrate how often to reapply.
  • Roses: Stop deadheading by Sept 1. Allowing rose hips to form signals the plant to harden off for winter. Continued deadheading pushes tender late growth that won't harden before frost.
  • Perennial Bed: Cut back most spent perennial stems. Leave some seed heads for overwintering birds (coneflowers, black-eyed susans are especially good). Leave grasses standing.
💊 Spray Schedule — September BED 2 + ROSE BED
  • Roses: You can reduce or stop Immunox sprays by mid-September as the season winds down. A final copper spray can help harden off canes.
  • Bed 2 — Fall disease prevention: Apply a final copper spray to all cane fruits and currants as leaves begin to turn — reduces disease overwintering on canes. This is a cleanup spray, not a growing-season application.
  • Bed 1 tomatoes: If plants are still productive, continue Mancozeb/copper rotation through harvest.
  • 🪲 Cucumber beetles (Bed 3 + Pots) — TAPERING BUT PRESENT: Beetle activity drops in September as temps cool but adults remain active until the first hard frost, especially on warm days. Continue bifenthrin every 10–14 days as long as cucumbers are still producing. Once you pull the plants for the season, adult beetles will disperse to overwinter in nearby leaf litter and wood edges — this is why cleaning up bed debris promptly in fall slightly reduces next year's population.
  • Other spray programs wind down significantly in September — cool nights reduce fungal pressure on most crops.
🌍 Soil Prep & Amendments BEDS 1 + 4
  • As Bed 1 plants finish, remove spent plants and debris — don't leave diseased tomato plants to overwinter in the bed.
  • Apply 2–3" of compost to cleared Bed 1 areas. Work in lightly. Bed can rest over winter with compost mulching and breaking down.
  • Apply ground eggshells (calcium supplement) to Bed 1 now — they take months to break down, so fall application feeds the soil by spring. Good use for this slow‑release amendment.
  • Soil test now if you haven't — results inform your fall lime/amendment decisions. NJ Cooperative Extension can test for pH and nutrients inexpensively.
  • If pH is low: apply agricultural lime in fall so it has time to integrate before spring.
🍓 Strawberry Bed — Fall Care BED 2
  • If planting new strawberries this fall: plant now (Sept 1–30) for establishment before winter.
  • Renovated plants from August should now have fresh new foliage — keep beds weeded. Apply light Plant‑Tone if not done in August.
  • Apply Preen around strawberry plants for weed suppression if you've been managing runners manually. Don't apply where you want runners to root.

FALL — Frost Season
❄️
First Frost ~Oct 15–20
Keep row cover or old bedsheets ready. A light frost (28–32°F) can damage tomatoes and peppers but often a single frost is followed by a week of warmth. Cover for light frosts; harvest everything before a hard frost (below 28°F for multiple hours).
🍂 Final Harvests ALL BEDS
  • Before first hard frost: harvest ALL remaining tomatoes, peppers, and eggplant. Peppers can be frozen (cut and freeze raw). Green tomatoes ripen on the countertop.
  • Carrots (Bed 3): Harvest before ground freezes hard (late October/early November). The light frosts of October actually sweeten them — leave until you need the space or frost risk is serious.
  • Swiss chard and lettuce (Bed 4): Continue harvesting; both handle light frost. Swiss chard can go until hard freeze. Cover with row cover for extended harvest into November.
  • Herbs (Pots): Bring tender rosemary indoors before frost — it's marginally hardy in Zone 6b but safer to overwinter inside (sunny window or grow lights). Thyme and mint can stay out; mint dies back but returns. Parsley can handle a frost or two.
✂️ Cut-Back & Cleanup BEDS 1 + 3 + 4
  • Peonies: After first hard frost, cut stems to 1" above soil level. Remove ALL foliage from the bed — peony botrytis overwinters in old leaves. Do NOT compost peony debris.
  • Beds 1 and 4: Remove all spent annual plants after harvest/frost kills them. Pull roots where possible (disease reduction). Add to compost if disease-free; bag and dispose if diseased (tomato blight, powdery mildew).
  • Perennial Bed: Leave ornamental grasses and seed heads standing through winter for bird habitat. Cut back the rest to 4–6".
  • Roses: Do NOT prune roses in fall in Zone 6b — this stimulates tender growth that winterkills. Wait for spring. After 2–3 hard frosts, mound 8–12" of soil and/or compost around the base of each rose for winter protection.
🌍 Soil Amendment & Mulch ALL BEDS
  • Add 2–3" of compost to all cleared beds. This is the most important thing you can do for next year's garden — winter decomposition incorporates organic matter deep into the soil.
  • Lightly till or fork compost into the top 4–6" of empty beds — or just lay it on top (no-till approach). Both work; no-till preserves soil structure and biology better.
  • Ground eggshells: Apply to beds now (slow‑release calcium; it takes months to break down and be available).
  • Apply lime if pH is below 6.0 (based on soil test). Fall application allows winter rains to work it in.
  • Garlic (Bed 3): After mulching in October, add more straw (total 4–6") once hard freezes arrive (late Oct/Nov). Don't over-mulch too early — wait for the ground to begin freezing.
🫐 Berry Bed Winterizing BED 2
  • Strawberries: Mulch with 2–3" of straw AFTER the first hard frost (late October or early November). Mulching too early insulates soil warmth and delays dormancy — wait for the plants to truly go dormant.
  • All cane fruits: Ensure all fruited canes are removed. Tie remaining primocanes to trellis for wind support.
  • Currants/Gooseberries: No special winterizing needed — they're cold-hardy. Ensure mulch around the base is 2–3" of wood chips to protect the root zone.

November
Closing time — clean up, put the garden to bed, give thanks
LATE FALL — Garden Sleeps
🌍 Final Soil & Mulch Work ALL BEDS
  • Top-dress all beds with a final 2" of shredded leaves or wood chips if compost application wasn't enough. Bare soil is vulnerable to erosion and nutrient loss over winter.
  • Work any remaining blood meal or bone meal into empty beds now — they'll break down slowly over winter and be available in spring.
  • Increase garlic straw mulch to 4–6" as ground begins to freeze (typically mid-November in NJ).
  • Mound rose bases with 10–12" of soil/compost (a "rose cone" of soil). Burlap wrapping is optional; soil mounding is essential in Zone 6b.
  • Final strawberry straw mulch increase if not done in October.
🔧 Equipment Winterization ALL BEDS
  • Bed 1 drip tape dig-up: Before the ground freezes, flush the entire tape system with plain water for 10+ minutes — this clears any fertilizer salts or residue left in the lines from fertigation, which would otherwise dry and clog emitters in storage. Then dig up the tape, coil loosely, and store indoors above freezing. Label which rows are which if you have multiple lines. Inspect for cracks or damage while it's clean and on the bench — replace sections now rather than discovering failures in April.
  • Drain and store all hoses and irrigation fittings — frozen water expands and cracks couplings and solenoid valves. If the solenoid valve is exposed, bring it inside or insulate it.
  • Clean all spray equipment thoroughly — residual chemicals damage seals and pumps. Flush with clean water 2–3 times, then run clean water through the wand. Store sprayers empty and slightly open.
  • Oil all metal tool surfaces; store in a dry location (rust destroys tools quickly).
  • Store chemicals above freezing — freezing temperatures degrade many liquid formulations. A basement or garage that stays above 35°F is ideal.
  • Store remaining liquid fertilizers (fish emulsion, Plant‑Tone liquid) where they won't freeze.
💊 Final Spray — Cane Fruits BED 2
  • Apply a final dormant-season copper spray to cane fruits before full dormancy (before leaves completely drop). This reduces disease inoculum overwintering on canes.
  • You can also apply a diluted lime-sulfur spray (lime-sulfur is a powerful dormant fungicide) at this stage. However, copper alone will serve you well.
  • No more insecticide sprays needed until spring.
♻️ Compost & Cleanup ALL BEDS
  • Add all disease-free plant debris to your compost pile (turn and add to center where heat will be greatest). Your daily coffee grounds should be going in here year-round — in November, add a big batch if you've been stockpiling. Coffee grounds are "green" (nitrogen-rich) material; balance with plenty of dried leaves or shredded cardboard.
  • Bag and remove diseased plant material (especially tomato blight, powdery mildew, rose cuttings). Do NOT compost these.
  • Shred fallen leaves with the lawn mower — shredded leaves break down faster in compost and are excellent as bed mulch.
  • Cover compost pile with a tarp to prevent leaching of nutrients over wet winter months.

🐝 Bee Safety — Critical Rules

All pyrethroids (bifenthrin, lambda-cyhalothrin/Sevin), malathion, and neem oil are toxic to bees to varying degrees. Always follow these rules:

→ Spray in the early morning or late evening when bees are not actively foraging.
→ Never spray open flowers on any plant. If a plant is in bloom, skip it or hand-remove pests.
→ Insecticidal soap and copper spray have the lowest bee toxicity — prefer these when pollinators are active.
→ Captain Jack's Deadbug (Spinosad) is moderately toxic when wet but safe once dry — spray at dusk.
→ Neem oil is low to moderate toxicity but can disrupt bee reproduction — minimize during heavy bloom periods.
